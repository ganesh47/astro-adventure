# Educational Progression

## Goal

Progression turns isolated astronomy facts into a sequence of observations, comparisons, and evidence-based explanations appropriate for ages 4-12.

## Progression Model

| Stage | Player action | Learning target | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Observe | Scan and name a visible clue | Notice one concrete feature | Mercury is closest to the Sun |
| Retrieve | Answer from a just-seen clue | Strengthen memory through recall | Pick the clue that identifies Mars |
| Compare | Sort or choose between destinations | Notice similarities and differences | Planet vs. moon, rocky vs. icy |
| Explain | Use evidence from a card or scene | Connect clue to reason | Mars looks red because of rusty iron in dust |
| Revisit | Meet the idea later in a new context | Spaced practice and transfer | Use prior scan data to plan a route |

## Content Anchors

- NASA facts are the primary source for destination content. Solar System facts: https://science.nasa.gov/solar-system/facts/
- NASA Space Place is preferred for kid-facing wording and early player copy drafts: https://spaceplace.nasa.gov/en/kid/planets/
- NGSS grade 5 space systems and middle school solar system comparison standards shape the age progression. Topic overview: https://www.nextgenscience.org/overview-topics and MS-ESS1-3: https://www.nextgenscience.org/pe/ms-ess1-3-earths-place-universe
- Retrieval and spaced revisit choices follow IES/WWC learning guidance on active retrieval: https://ies.ed.gov/ncee/wwc/PracticeGuide.aspx?sid=1

## First Destination Sequence

- Mercury: location in the solar system, close-to-Sun clue, high-level orbit concept.
- Mars: surface color clue, rusty iron dust, comparison with Earth as a rocky planet.
- Europa: moon vs. planet distinction, Jupiter connection, icy surface and ocean-interest clue.

## Requirements

- Every mission introduces no more than three required science facts.
- Every fact has one observation moment and one retrieval or reflection moment.
- Every later comparison uses previously introduced clues before adding new vocabulary.
- Assessment avoids pure trivia when a clue, model, image, or data card can support reasoning.

## Review Checklist

- Is the fact sourced by NASA, NGSS, or another approved public science source?
- Is the prompt age-appropriate for the selected age band without removing the science idea?
- Does the task ask the player to observe, retrieve, compare, explain, or revisit?
- Does feedback explain the concept without shame or pressure?
